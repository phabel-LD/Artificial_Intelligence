## Key Learnings and Insights

Perceptron and Activation Functions
- The perceptron is a linear classifier; activation functions (sigmoid, tanh, ReLU) introduce non‑linearity, enabling complex representations.
- The step function gives hard binary decisions; modern activation functions are differentiable, enabling gradient‑based learning.

Regression
- Linear and logistic regression are foundational supervised learning techniques.
- Logistic regression extends naturally to multiclass problems using one‑vs‑rest or softmax.
- Correlation analysis helps identify relevant features for predictive modelling.

Backpropagation
- Understanding gradient flow and weight initialisation is crucial for training neural networks.
- Random initialisation breaks symmetry and improves learning; zero initialisation causes all neurons to learn the same features.
- Mini‑batch gradient descent balances computational efficiency and convergence stability.

Dropout
- Dropout is an effective regulariser that prevents co‑adaptation of neurons.
- It forces the network to learn redundant representations, improving generalisation.
- Dropout rates between 0.2 and 0.5 are commonly used in practice.

CNNs vs MLPs
- CNNs preserve spatial structure and are parameter‑efficient.
- MLPs flatten images and lose spatial information, making them suitable only for small, centred datasets like MNIST.
- Convolutional layers learn local features (edges, textures) that are shared across the image.

CNN Filters
- Convolutional filters act as feature detectors; early layers detect edges, later layers detect complex patterns.
- Visualising activation maps helps interpret what a CNN has learned.
- Filters can be manually designed or learned from data.

Data Augmentation
- Geometric and color transformations increase effective dataset size, improving generalisation and robustness.
- Augmentations should be chosen to reflect realistic variations in the target domain.
- Common augmentations include flips, rotations, crops, brightness, contrast, and hue adjustments.

Transfer Learning
- Pre‑trained models (e.g., ResNet) provide strong feature extractors that can be fine‑tuned with very small datasets.
- Transfer learning significantly reduces training time and data requirements.
- It is the standard approach for computer vision tasks with limited labelled data.

Business & Research Applications:
- Image classification and object detection.
- Predictive analytics (regression and classification).
- Model interpretability and debugging.
- Building robust AI systems with limited labelled data.
