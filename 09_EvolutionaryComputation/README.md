## Notebooks Overview

### 01 – Genetic Algorithm for Constrained Optimisation
Topic: Constrained optimisation using a penalty approach.
Problem: Formulate a nutritional bar that meets protein, carbohydrate, fat, and calorie requirements at minimum cost.
Key Techniques: Binary chromosome encoding, soft penalty for constraint violations, roulette wheel selection, single‑point crossover, bit‑flip mutation, elitism.
Visualisation: Convergence plots, bar charts.
Applications: Product formulation, resource allocation, engineering design.

### 02 – Genetic Algorithm for Production Optimisation
Topic: Maximising profit under capacity and demand constraints.
Problem: Determine optimal production quantities for multiple products.
Key Techniques: Integer chromosome encoding, hard and multiplicative penalty functions, tournament selection, single‑point crossover, creep and random‑reset mutation.
Visualisation: Convergence comparison, bar charts.
Applications: Manufacturing planning, supply chain management, operations research.

### 03 – Genetic Algorithm for Multi‑Objective Optimisation (NSGA‑II)
Topic: Multi‑objective optimisation with Pareto fronts.
Problems: Dent problem (bi‑objective test function) and portfolio optimisation (return vs. risk).
Key Techniques: Non‑dominated sorting, crowding distance, elitism (NSGA‑II), custom crossover/mutation for sum‑to‑one constraints.
Visualisation: Scatter plots of feasible space and Pareto fronts.
Applications: Financial portfolio management, engineering trade‑off analysis, decision support.

### 04 – Genetic Algorithm for Box Optimisation
Topic: Constrained optimisation of a box volume with binary encoding.
Problem: Maximise volume subject to surface area ≤ 20.
Key Techniques: Binary chromosome (6 bits per variable), soft penalty, tournament selection, two‑point crossover, gene‑wise mutation, elitism.
Visualisation: Convergence plot.
Applications: Geometric design, packaging optimisation, structural engineering.

### 05 – Genetic Algorithm for Function Minimisation
Topic: Continuous function minimisation using binary encoding.
Problem: Minimise a multi‑modal function f(x, y) = 20 + x² + y² − cos(2πx) + cos(2πy) on [-5.12, 5.12]².
Key Techniques: Binary encoding (16 bits per variable), fitness transformation 1/(1+f), tournament selection, single‑point crossover, bit‑flip mutation, elitist replacement.
Visualisation: Density plot, 3D surface and contour plots, convergence scatter.
Applications: Global optimisation, hyperparameter tuning, benchmark testing.

## Key Learnings and Insights

Constraint Handling:
- Soft penalties are more effective than hard rejection because they allow infeasible solutions to contribute genetic material, maintaining diversity.
- The penalty factor should be tuned to balance constraint satisfaction and objective optimisation.

Encoding:
- Binary encoding is intuitive and works well for discrete or continuous variables when the range and precision are known.
- Real‑valued encoding with simulated binary crossover (SBX) may be more efficient for continuous problems.

Selection Pressure:
- Tournament selection (size 3) provides a good balance between exploration and exploitation.
- Roulette wheel selection can lead to premature convergence if fitness values are highly skewed.

Crossover and Mutation:
- Two‑point crossover and gene‑wise mutation introduce greater diversity than single‑point crossover and bit‑flip mutation.
- For constrained problems, custom operators that maintain feasibility (e.g., normalisation) are essential.

Population Size:
- Larger populations (50–250 individuals) improve exploration but increase computational cost.
- A population size of 50–100 was sufficient for most problems; larger populations (250) were needed for rugged landscapes.

Elitism:
- Preserving the best few individuals guarantees monotonic improvement.
- Combining elitism with a large population mitigates the loss of diversity.

Multi‑objective Optimisation:
- NSGA‑II effectively handles conflicting objectives, producing well‑distributed Pareto fronts.
- Crowding distance preserves diversity across the Pareto front.

Convergence:
- Most problems converged within 100–250 generations.
- Early generations show rapid improvement, followed by fine‑tuning and stabilisation.

Business & Research Applications:
- Product formulation and cost minimisation.
- Production planning under capacity constraints.
- Portfolio optimisation and risk management.
- Engineering design and geometric optimisation.
- Benchmarking and algorithm comparison.
