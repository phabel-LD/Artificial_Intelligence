Intelligent Agents:
- Agents follow the sense‑think‑act cycle; even simple agents can solve meaningful tasks (recommendation, monitoring, comparison).
- Collaborative filtering relies on similarity metrics; the choice of metric affects results.
- Observer agents are fundamental for logging and debugging in larger systems.

Uninformed Search:
- DFS is memory‑efficient but does not guarantee the shortest path; it can get trapped in deep branches.
- BFS guarantees the shortest path for unweighted problems but consumes more memory.
- The 8‑puzzle is an excellent benchmark for comparing search strategies.

Informed Search:
- A* with Manhattan distance is significantly more efficient than BFS, pruning unpromising branches early.
- The heuristic must be admissible (never overestimate) to guarantee optimality.
- More informed heuristics (e.g., linear conflict) further reduce the search space.

Simulated Annealing:
- The cooling schedule (alpha) and initial temperature control exploration vs. exploitation.
- The algorithm can escape local minima due to probabilistic acceptance of worse solutions.
- Parameter tuning is crucial; insufficient iterations can trap the algorithm in local minima.

Adversarial Search:
- Minimax assumes optimal play from both players; it is the foundation of game‑playing AI.
- Alpha‑beta pruning reduces the search space from O(b^d) to O(b^(d/2)) in the best case.
- The evaluation function (heuristic) is critical; simple line‑based scoring is sufficient for Tic‑Tac‑Toe.

Business & Research Applications:
- Search methods are used in route planning, scheduling, resource allocation, game AI, and optimisation across industries.
- Heuristic search and metaheuristics are essential for NP‑hard problems.
